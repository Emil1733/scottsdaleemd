THE "SINKING" PROBLEM

Why Your Yard Sinks
(And How We Stop It)

When dirt is simply dumped into a pool cavity, it contains millions of micro-voids (air pockets). Over time, gravity and desert monsoons cause the soil to collapse into these pockets. This is called "settlement," and it ruins landscaping investment.

🛠️ **The 12-Inch Lift Method**: We never fill the whole pool at once. We fill in specific 12-inch layers called "lifts," compacting each one with mechanical vibrating plates before moving to the next.
💧 **Moisture Conditioning**: Dry Arizona soil won't compact. We optimize the moisture level of every lift using high-precision water application to achieve maximum Proctor density.
📈 **95% Proctor Standard**: This is the benchmark required by Maricopa City Engineers. It ensures the ground is as stable as—or more stable than—undisturbed native soil.

GEOTECHNICAL SCIENCE

The Physics of a Stable Backfill

To prevent settlement in Scottsdale, we must manage the microscopic environment of the soil. This requires a deep understanding of soil mechanics and material science.

The Anatomy of Soil Voids

Soil is not a solid mass; it is a collection of individual particles. In its uncompacted state, soil contains a high volume of "voids"—pockets of air and water between the particles. When a contractor simply dumps dirt into a pool hole, these voids can make up as much as 30% of the total volume. Over time, as gravity and groundwater move through the fill, the particles rearrange themselves to fill these voids. This rearrangement is what causes the ground to "sink" or "settle." Our engineering goal is to mechanically eliminate these voids *before* your landscaping is installed.

Particle Size Distribution (PSD)

Not all dirt is created equal. For a structural backfill, we utilize "well-graded" soil—a mix of particle sizes from fine silts to coarse sands. The science behind this is simple but critical: the smaller particles fill the spaces between the larger particles, creating a dense, interlocking matrix. This "interlocking" is what provides the soil with its load-bearing capacity. If you use "uniform" soil (where all particles are the same size), it will never achieve 95% density, regardless of how much you compact it.

Vibratory Impact & Shear Strength

We achieve this interlocking through "Vibratory Compaction." By applying high-frequency vibration to each 12-inch lift, we temporarily reduce the friction between soil particles, allowing them to slide into their most dense configuration. This process increases the soil's "Shear Strength"—the property that allows it to resist shifting or collapsing under the weight of an ADU, a heavy stone patio, or even a luxury swimming pool replacement.

MOISTURE CONTROL

Achieving 95% Density
in the Arizona Heat

The biggest challenge to backfill in Scottsdale is the climate. Arizona soil is naturally "bone dry," which prevents it from compacting properly. To achieve structural density, we must engage in precise moisture-conditioning.

Optimum Moisture Content (OMC)

Every soil type has an "Optimum Moisture Content"—the exact percentage of water (by weight) that allows for the highest possible density. If the soil is too dry, the particles create friction and won't settle. If it's too wet, the water itself takes up space (pore pressure), preventing compaction. We use high-pressure atomizing nozzles to condition each lift to within 1% of its lab-tested OMC before the vibratory rollers ever touch the dirt.

Moisture Control Specs

  • ✓ In-Situ Hydration: Real-time soil moisture monitoring during fill.
  • ✓ Saturation Prevention: Ensuring zero "pumping" or soil elasticity.
  • ✓ Lift Curing: Managing evaporation during high-heat operations.
  • ✓ Nuclear Verification: Final moisture/density check per lift.

Structural Resilience: Engineering for Dynamic Loads

A common oversight in Scottsdale pool removals is only engineering for "Static Loads" (the weight of the building itself). However, a truly build-ready site must also withstand "Dynamic Loads"—the pressure exerted by moving heavy equipment, the vibration of nearby traffic, and the sudden weight of high-volume monsoon water. Our engineered backfill utilizes a "Stress-Stiffening" approach, where the particle interlocking is calibrated to increase in strength under pressure. This ensures that when your general contractor brings in a 10-ton forklift or a heavy concrete truck for your new Casita, the ground under their tires remains perfectly stable, preventing the subterranean shifting that causes foundation cracks years later. This is the difference between a "Landscape Fill" and an "Arroyo Structural Pad."

COMPACTION FAQ

Technical Stability Questions

Can I use the dirt from the pool excavation as backfill?

Generally, no. In Scottsdale, the material removed during pool construction often contains large chunks of caliche or high-plasticity clay that will not compact to structural standards. We utilize "Imported Clean Fill"—soil that has been screened and tested to ensure it meets the well-graded specifications required for a 95% certified pad. Using native "spoils" is the #1 cause of settlement issues.

What is a "Nuclear Density Gauge"?

A nuclear gauge is the industrial standard for verifying soil compaction. It emits a small amount of gamma radiation into the soil and measures how much of it is reflected back. This allows the geotechnical engineer to calculate the soil's dry density and moisture content in real-time, providing the data needed for a legal certification. We coordinate this 3rd-party verification for all of our structural projects.

Why is 95% the "Magic Number"?

95% of the "Modified Proctor" maximum density is the structural threshold recognized by the International Building Code (IBC) and local Scottsdale ordinances. At 95% density, the soil is structurally indistinguishable from virgin ground, meaning it can support the weight of concrete foundations, heavy stone walls, and other permanent improvements without settling.

How long does the compaction process take?

For a standard 15,000-gallon pool, the engineered backfill process typically takes 2 to 3 full days. This includes the time required for moisture-conditioning, mechanical vibration of multiple 12-inch lifts, and on-site testing by the geotechnical engineering firm. While it's slower than "dumping and filling," it's the only way to ensure the site is build-ready.

What happens to the water table under the fill?

This is a critical engineering point. In Scottsdale, the "perched water table" can create hydrostatic pressure if the bottom of the old pool shell is not properly perforated. We utilize industrial-grade core drills to create a grid of 18-inch drainage ports through the floor of the excavation. This ensures that groundwater can move freely through the sub-surface, preventing the "bathtub effect" where water collects under your new backfill and liquefies the soil. By managing this pressure, we ensure the long-term stability of the entire site.
