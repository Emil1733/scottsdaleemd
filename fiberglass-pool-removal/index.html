Why Fiberglass Doesn't Break Like Concrete

A fiberglass pool is essentially a single massive "tub" of reinforced resin. You cannot simply "fracture" it for drainage like a concrete shell. It must be surgically sectioned and physically extracted from the ground. If left in place and filled (Partial Demo), the shell can eventually "float" or "pop" during Scottsdale's heavy monsoon seasons due to subterranean water pressure. Proper drainage management is critical during this process.

The Buoyancy Factor: Why Fiberglass Removal is Dangerous

In the expansive clay soils of Scottsdale and Paradise Valley, a fiberglass pool is essentially a giant underground boat. To remove it correctly, we must first defeat the physics of buoyancy.

The "Bathtub Effect" & Site Collapse

Concrete pools are heavy enough to stay anchored by their own mass. Fiberglass pools, however, are light and flexible. In many Scottsdale neighborhoods, the soil has a high clay content that traps water against the outside of the shell. This creates **Hydrostatic Pressure**. If a contractor drains the pool and begins digging without first stabilizing this pressure, the shell can "pop" or heave upward, causing immediate collapse of the surrounding soil and potentially undermining the foundations of your home or patio. We utilize specialized sub-surface relief valves and perimeter dewatering protocols to ensure the site remains structurally balanced during every stage of the extraction.

Surgical Sectioning vs. The "Big Rip"

Many "Quick-Lube" demolition crews try to rip a fiberglass shell out in one piece using heavy excavators. This is a recipe for disaster. The sudden release of tension can send fiberglass shards flying and create a massive, unmanaged void that is impossible to compact. We utilize diamond-tip industrial saws to section the shell into manageable 4-foot by 8-foot panels. This allow us to maintain "Side-Wall Tension" as we backfill, ensuring that the ground never has a chance to shift or settle during the removal process.

The Chemical Integrity of the Sub-Soil

Fiberglass pools are manufactured using polyester resins and gel coats. Over decades, if a shell is cracked during a "Partial Demo," these chemicals can slowly leach into the surrounding soil, affecting the pH of your yard and potentially contaminating groundwater. By performing a **100% Extraction**, we eliminate the long-term environmental liability of decaying resin. This is particularly important for Scottsdale homeowners looking to convert their pool area into a high-end organic garden or a certified "Build-Ready" ADU pad.

SUB-SURFACE STABILITY

The Hidden Danger of Pea Gravel

Most fiberglass pools installed in Scottsdale were backfilled with "pea gravel" to allow for shell flexibility. While this works for a functional pool, it is a structural nightmare for a former pool area. Pea gravel is "non-compactable" and acts like millions of tiny ball bearings under the soil.

Why Gravel Removal is Mandatory

If you leave the pea gravel in place and dump soil on top, the gravel will eventually shift, leading to sudden sinkholes and surface cracking. We perform a "Full Void Remediation," where we extract every cubic yard of the original gravel fill and replace it with engineered structural soil, compacted to 95% density. This is the only way to ensure your yard is safe for future construction or heavy hardscaping.

Environmental Stewardship: The Fiberglass Recycling Protocol

Unlike concrete, which is easily crushed into road base, fiberglass is a complex composite material that requires specialized handling. At Arroyo Excavation, we adhere to the highest environmental standards for fiberglass disposal. Instead of simply dumping the shell sections into a standard landfill, we coordinate with industrial waste processors who can repurpose the fiberglass panels for use in reinforced concrete products or as fuel in high-temperature industrial kilns. FIBERGLASS FAQ

Technical Extraction Questions

Can a crane lift the pool in one piece?

While possible, it is rarely the safest or most cost-effective option. Lifting a full shell (which can weigh several tons with attached plumbing and mud) over a multi-million dollar Scottsdale home carries significant risk. We typically section the shell into 4-6 large panels, which can then be lifted safely and efficiently by a smaller, more nimble crane or even a specialized loader, reducing the risk of property damage to near zero.

What happens if the shell is already cracked?

If a shell is already compromised, the risk of "Hydrostatic Heave" is actually lower, but the risk of soil contamination and leaching is higher. In these cases, we prioritize immediate containment of the site. We extract the broken panels carefully to ensure that no fiberglass shards or resin dust are left behind in the soil, followed by a full "Clean-Fill" replacement of the entire excavation area.

Do I need a special permit for fiberglass?

The primary permit is the standard City of Scottsdale Pool Decommissioning permit. However, because fiberglass is a "Non-Biodegradable Industrial Waste," we must also provide documentation to the city and county showing that the material was disposed of at a licensed industrial facility, not a standard landfill. We handle all of this "Cradle-to-Grave" documentation for you.

Is fiberglass dust dangerous for my neighbors?

Raw fiberglass dust is an irritant to the lungs and skin. To protect your family and neighbors, we utilize "Wet-Saw" technology during all sectioning operations. By continually applying high-pressure water to the diamond blades, we trap the fiberglass particles in a slurry, which is then vacuumed up and disposed of safely, ensuring zero airborne particulate matter.

FAQ

Fiberglass Removal Questions

Can I just fill in a fiberglass pool?

It is strongly discouraged in Arizona. A "Partial" fiberglass removal is prone to sinking and "The Bathtub Effect" where the shell traps water, making the yard permanently swampy. We recommend 100% extraction for fiberglass.

How is the shell disposed of?

Fiberglass is not biodegradable and cannot go into standard construction recycling. We transport the sectioned shell to certified Maricopa hazardous/industrial waste facilities for proper ecological disposal.

Will this damage my patio?

By use of "Shield & Lift" techniques, we can often extract shells while only removing the immediate 12–24 inches of surrounding deck, preserving as much of your existing patio as possible.

Is it more expensive than concrete?

Actually, often less! While the extraction logic is more complex, the material volume is lower, reducing hauling and disposal costs compared to massive concrete shells.
