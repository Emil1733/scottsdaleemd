Decommissioning the 1970s Plaster Standard

McCormick Ranch was the first major master-planned community in Scottsdale. Many of its original pool shells are now facing "End-of-Life" structural failure.

Concrete Fatigue & Rebar Corrosion

The pool shells constructed in Central Scottsdale during the 1970s and 80s were built before modern structural concrete standards were fully implemented. Over 40+ years, the exposure to chlorinated water and Arizona's thermal expansion cycles leads to **Concrete Fatigue**. Once the water penetrates the plaster and hits the structural rebar, it causes the metal to oxidize (rust) and expand. This internal pressure creates massive cracks that cannot be permanently patched. We specialize in the full extraction of these compromised shells. By removing the decaying rebar and "fatigued" concrete, we eliminate the risk of subterranean rust leaching into your soil, providing a clean slate for your new backyard environment.

Legacy Plumbing: The "Dead-Leg" Risk

Older pools in neighborhoods like Gainey Ranch and McCormick Ranch often utilize outdated copper or thin-wall PVC plumbing. If these lines are not "Surgically Safed-Off" at the source during a pool removal, they can become "Dead-Legs"—trapped lines that hold stagnant water and eventually leak, creating sinkholes under your new landscaping. Our decommissioning protocol involves a full pressure-test of all abandoned lines and a source-capping at the main utility junction. This ensures that your yard remains dry and stable for decades to come.

Managing the Central Scottsdale Water Table

McCormick Ranch is famous for its man-made lakes and lush greenbelts. However, this proximity to water means that the local water table is often much higher than in the surrounding desert. A pool removal that doesn't account for this can lead to "Hydrostatic Uplift" or permanent swampy conditions in your yard.

The "Bathtub Effect" Prevention

If a pool shell is simply filled with dirt without proper perforation, it acts as a subterranean "bathtub" that collects irrigation runoff and rainwater. In the high-moisture environment of McCormick Ranch, this leads to soil liquefaction and the eventual collapse of your yard's surface. We utilize a "High-Density Perforation" protocol, punching 12-inch drainage ports through the pool floor every 4 feet. This is followed by a layer of permeable aggregate to ensure that water can move naturally through the soil column, preventing any "soft spots" or drainage issues near your home's foundation.
